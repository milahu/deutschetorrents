  2) Radical critics of industrial/civilized mass society (with quick notes)

    Directly relevant critics

      Jacques Ellul — The Technological Society: penetrating critique of technological determinism and the ways technique shapes human life and institutions. Very relevant to anti-industrial viewpoints.

      Ivan Illich — Tools for Conviviality: criticizes institutional overreach and advocates human-scale tools, convivial institutions, and local autonomy.

      Christopher Ryan — Civilized to Death: a contemporary, polemical critique arguing civilization often harmed health and social life compared to hunter-gatherer communities.

      Murray Bookchin — social ecology / libertarian municipalism: radical decentralization, ecological society, direct democracy — explicitly anti-state and anti-hierarchical; strongly pro nonviolent community building.

      Peter Kropotkin — Mutual Aid: classical anarchist argument that cooperation is foundational in nature; supports decentralization and community-based organization.

      Albert Camus / Existentialists (Camus, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche’s existential interpreters): dealing with mortality honestly while rejecting nihilistic violence; valuable on the psychology of courage and revolt.

    Controversial / cautionary picks

      Friedrich Nietzsche: critique of “herd morality” and praise of creative life. Philosophically useful, but his aphorisms have often been misused to justify domination — interpret carefully.

      Martin Heidegger: deep critique of technological enframing; philosophically rich but politically and historically complicated (Nazism ties).

      Ayn Rand / Ludwig von Mises: both are radical anti-collectivist critics of socialism and mass politics. Rand celebrates a kind of heroic individualism (objectivism); Mises defends free-market liberalism and critiques state planning. They’ll resonate if you prize individual strength and critique mass institutions — but they don’t advance tribalist or anti-industrial ecology and often celebrate market structures you seem to dislike.

    Other useful critics

      Christopher Lasch — The Revolt of the Elites: critique of cultural decadence and managerial elites.

      Jacque Derrida / Michel Foucault (for understanding power and discourse — useful tactically, not a program).

      Aldo Leopold & permaculture writers (for practical, ecological, local living).

      Elinor Ostrom — Nobel laureate on how communities successfully manage commons with decentralized rules (practical, academically respected).
